4. Suppose \(Y_1, Y_2, \dots, Y_n\) are i.i.d. \(Weibull(\gamma, \alpha, \beta)\) random variables, where \(- \infty < \gamma < \infty, \ \alpha > 0\), and \(\beta > 0\), with density \[ f(y \ \big| \ \gamma, \alpha, \beta) = \frac{\alpha}{\beta^{\alpha}}(y - \gamma)^{\alpha - 1} \exp \bigg[ - \bigg( \frac{y - \gamma}{\beta}\bigg)^{\alpha} \bigg] \] for \(y > 0\) and \(0\) otherwise. Note that \[ \begin{aligned} E[Y] &= \gamma + \beta \ \Gamma \bigg(\frac{1}{\alpha} + 1 \bigg) \\ \text{Var}(Y) &= \beta^2 \bigg[ \Gamma \bigg(\frac{2}{\alpha} + 1 \bigg) - \Gamma \bigg(\frac{1}{\alpha} + 1 \bigg)^2 \bigg] \end{aligned} \] Assume the location parameter \(\gamma = 0\). The shape parameter is \(\alpha\) and the parameter \(\beta\) is the scale parameter.

  1. Determine the likelihood function of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\), \(L(\alpha, \beta)\).
    Solution
    \[ \begin{align*} L(\alpha, \beta) &= f(y_1, \dots, y_n \ | \ \alpha, \beta) \\ &= \prod_{i=1}^n f(y_i | \alpha, \beta) \\ &= \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{\alpha}{\beta^{\alpha}}y_i^{\alpha - 1} \exp \left\{- \left( \frac{y_i}{\beta}\right)^{\alpha} \right\} \\ &= \frac{\alpha^n}{\beta^{n \alpha}} \left( \prod_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha -1} \right) \exp \left\{ - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{y_i^{\alpha}}{\beta^{\alpha}} \right\} \end{align*} \]

  2. Determine the log-likelihood function of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\), \(\ell(\alpha, \beta)\).
    Solution
    \[ \begin{align*} \ell (\alpha, \beta) &= \log L(\alpha, \beta) \\ &= n \log \alpha - n \alpha \log \beta + (\alpha - 1) \sum_{i=1}^n \log y_i - \frac{1}{\beta^{\alpha}} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \end{align*} \]

  3. Show the two equations that need to be satisfied to compute the maximum likelihood estimators (MLEs), \(\hat{\alpha}\) and \(\hat{\beta}\), of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\), respectively.
    Solution
    \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} \ell (\alpha, \beta) = 0\)
    \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \ell (\alpha, \beta) = 0\)
    \[ \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \ell (\alpha, \beta) &= - \frac{n \alpha}{\beta} + \alpha \beta^{- \alpha -1} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ 0 &= - \frac{n \alpha}{\beta} + \alpha \beta^{- \alpha -1} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ \frac{n \alpha}{\beta} &= \alpha \beta^{- \alpha -1} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ n \alpha &= \beta \cdot \alpha \beta^{- \alpha -1} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ n \alpha &= \alpha \frac{1}{\beta^{\alpha}} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ n \alpha \cdot \beta^{\alpha} &= \alpha \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ \beta^{\alpha} &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \\ \hat{\beta} &= \left( \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \right)^{1/\alpha} \end{align*} \] Before determining the MLE of \(\alpha\), note: \[ \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} y^{\alpha} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} \exp \{ \log y^{\alpha} \} \\ &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} \exp \{ \alpha \log y \} \\ &= \exp \{ \alpha \log y \} \cdot \log y \\ &= \exp \{ \log y^{\alpha} \} \cdot \log y \\ &= y^{\alpha} \log y \end{align*} \] (Unclear why that was necessary). \[ \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} \ell (\alpha, \beta) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \alpha} \Bigg[ n \log \alpha - n \alpha \log \beta + (\alpha - 1) \sum_{i=1}^n \log y_i - \frac{1}{\beta^{\alpha}} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \Bigg] \\ &= \frac{n}{\alpha} - n \log \beta + \sum_{i=1}^n \log y_i - \frac{1}{\beta^{\alpha}} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i^{\alpha} \log y_i \tag*{(Need to apply chain rule to last term)} \\ \end{align*} \] Then plug in \(\hat{\beta}\) and solve for \(\alpha\) . . .

# Test H_0: beta = 5 versus H_1: beta != 5

  1. Consider two Weibull distributions to compare, \(Weibull (\alpha = 1, \beta = 5)\) and \(Weibull (\alpha = 1, \beta = 7)\), which model is more likely?
    Solution
    \(Weibull (\alpha = 1, \beta = 5)\)
    \[ \begin{align*} \log L(\beta = 5) &> \log L(\beta=7) \\ -276 &> -277 \end{align*} \]

  8. If \(Y_1, Y_2, \dots, Y_n\) are i.i.d. \(Exp(\beta)\), where \(\beta > 0\), with density \[ f(y \ | \ \beta) = \frac{1}{\beta} \exp \bigg[ - \bigg(\frac{y}{\beta} \bigg) \bigg] \] for \(y > 0\) and \(0\) otherwise.

    Determine the MLE of \(\beta\), the asymptotic variance (AV), and give a large sample 95% Confidence Interval for \(\beta\). Compute the interval, for the simuated data \(y\). Compare your answer with the interval used in part (l).
    Solution
    \(Y_1, \dots, Y_n \stackrel{iid}{\sim} Exp(\beta)\)
    \(f(y_i | \beta) = \frac{1}{\beta} \exp \{ -\frac{y}{\beta}\}\)
    \[ \begin{align*} L(\beta) &= f(y_1, \dots, y_n | \beta) \\ &= \prod_{i=1}^n f(y_i | \beta) \\ &= \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\beta} \exp \left\{ - \frac{y_i}{\beta} \right\} \\ &= \frac{1}{\beta^n} \exp \left\{ - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{y_i}{\beta} \right\} \\ \ell(\beta) &= \log \left( \frac{1}{\beta^n} \exp \left\{ - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{y_i}{\beta} \right\} \right) \\ &= -n \log \beta - \frac{1}{\beta} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}\ell(\beta) &= -\frac{n}{\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\ 0 &= -\frac{n}{\beta} + \frac{1}{\beta^2} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\ \frac{n}{\beta} &= \frac{1}{\beta^2} \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\ \hat{\beta} &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n y_i \\ &= \overline{y} \\ &= 5.79 \end{align*} \] Compute AV \[ \begin{align*} \log f(y | \beta) &= - \log \beta - \frac{y}{\beta} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta} \log f(y | \beta) &= - \frac{1}{\beta} + \frac{y}{\beta^2} \\ \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \beta^2} \log f(y | \beta) &= \frac{1}{\beta^2} - 2 \frac{y}{\beta^3} \end{align*} \] \[ \begin{align*} I(\beta) &= - E \left[ \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \beta^2} \log f(y | \beta) \right] \\ &= - E \left[ \frac{1}{\beta^2} - 2 \frac{Y}{\beta^3} \right] \\ &= - \frac{1}{\beta^2} + \frac{2}{\beta^3}E[Y] \\ &= - \frac{1}{\beta^2} + \frac{2}{\beta^3} \beta \\ &= \frac{1}{\beta^2} \end{align*} \] So \[ A.V. = \frac{1}{n I(\beta)} = \frac{\beta^2}{n} \] 95% confidence interval for \(\beta\): \[ \begin{align*} \hat{\beta} &\pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{\beta}^2}{n}} \\ \overline{y} &\pm 1.96 \frac{\overline{y}}{\sqrt{n}} = (4.65, 6.92) \end{align*} \]
